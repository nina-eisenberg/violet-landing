Lisa Chen HR Director
Hi Rob — wanted to follow up on the VP Engineering promotion. Carlos Vega has the most seniority and strongest performance reviews. Can you walk me through why you went with Tyler instead?

Tyler brings fresh energy and new perspectives to the role. We need someone who can keep up with the pace of modern engineering — cloud-native, AI-first thinking. Carlos is great but he's from a different era of engineering.
"Fresh energy" and "new perspectives" are age-coded language frequently cited in age discrimination cases. Courts have recognized these phrases as proxies for "younger."
"Different era" directly references the candidate's age/generational cohort and implies his experience is outdated rather than valuable.
Lisa Chen HR Director
Can you document the specific skills or qualifications where Tyler was stronger? I need to make sure this is defensible.
Rob Whitfield CTO
Sure — Tyler is more of a digital native, he lives and breathes the newer tech stack. Carlos is solid but you can tell he learned everything from books, not from building. It's just a different energy.
"Digital native" is inherently age-based — it refers to people who grew up with technology, which by definition excludes older workers. Using it as a job qualification is age discrimination.
Repeating the vague "energy" justification without specifics is a shifting explanation — a strong indicator of pretext in California employment law.

Module Debrief

Key Takeaways

  1. California's FEHA provides broader protections than federal Title VII — more protected categories, lower thresholds, and stronger remedies
  2. Quid pro quo harassment doesn't require an explicit demand — it's any link between job benefits and tolerance of unwelcome conduct
  3. Hostile work environment doesn't require "severe AND pervasive" under California law — severe OR pervasive is enough
  4. A single severe incident can constitute harassment — you don't need a pattern
  5. Harassment can occur between any parties: supervisor-to-subordinate, peer-to-peer, subordinate-to-supervisor, or third-party-to-employee
