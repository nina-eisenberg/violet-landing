How to Choose California Harassment Training That Actually Works

I'm going to say something that might sound strange coming from someone who sells harassment training: most harassment training is terrible.

I don't mean it's legally deficient, though some of it is. I mean it's terrible at doing the thing it's supposed to do: actually changing how people behave at work. Employees click through slides, answer obvious multiple-choice questions, collect their certificate, and forget everything by lunch. The company checks its compliance box. Nothing changes. And the next time someone gets harassed, the training didn't help.

I've investigated over 500 workplace harassment cases. In many of them, every single person involved had completed their required training. It didn't matter, because the training didn't stick.

So when I set out to build training for Violet, I started with the question most vendors never ask: what would actually make someone remember this?

What to look for

Real scenarios, not cartoons

The fastest way to spot bad training is the scenarios. If the harasser in the training is a mustache-twirling villain who says things no real person would say, the learner checks out. Real harassment is subtle. It's the manager who says "be more flexible with your time, if you know what I mean." It's the colleague who "keeps forgetting" your pronouns for three months. It's the client who puts his hand on your back at a networking event.

Your training should present situations where the right answer isn't immediately obvious, because that's what real life looks like. If every question has one cartoonishly wrong answer, one obviously right answer, and one filler answer, the learner isn't learning. They're pattern-matching.

California-specific content, not federal boilerplate

This one drives me crazy. I've reviewed training programs marketed as "California compliant" that barely mention FEHA. They teach federal Title VII standards, "severe AND pervasive," without explaining that California uses "severe OR pervasive." They skip the 18+ protected categories unique to California law. They don't mention the three-year CRD filing deadline, or that employees can skip the internal complaint process entirely and go straight to the state.

If your training was written for a national audience and then slapped with a "California Edition" label, your employees are learning the wrong legal standard. That's worse than no training at all, because they'll walk away confident in information that's incorrect.

Interactive means interactive

California's regulations at 2 CCR section 11024 require "effective interactive training." The regulation defines what that means: questions that assess learning, skill-building activities, and hypothetical scenarios with discussion questions.

A video your employees watch passively doesn't meet this standard, even if there's a quiz at the end. Interactive means the learner is making decisions, not just absorbing information. They should be choosing how to respond to a scenario, sorting conduct into categories, or building a response to a harassment disclosure, and getting feedback on their choices that explains the legal reasoning.

The trainer's qualifications matter

The regulations require that training be conducted by someone with knowledge and expertise in harassment prevention. That can be an attorney, an HR professional, or a harassment prevention consultant.

Ask your vendor: who created this content? If the answer is "our content team" with no named subject matter expert, that's a red flag. If the answer is a specific person with relevant credentials, that's what you want. The "attorney-created" distinction isn't marketing, it's the difference between content written by someone who has actually investigated harassment cases and content written by someone who read about them.

Auditable compliance mapping

Here's my attorney brain talking: if you can't point to exactly how your training satisfies each SB 1343 requirement, you don't actually know if you're compliant. You're trusting your vendor, and hope is not a compliance strategy.

Ask for a compliance map. A document that lists every requirement from Government Code section 12950.1 and 2 CCR section 11024, and shows you exactly where in the training each one is covered. If your vendor can't produce this, they either don't have it or don't want you to see the gaps.

I publish mine. Every requirement, mapped to specific training content. Because if you're asking employers to trust your training with their compliance, you should be willing to show your work.

What to avoid

"Micro-learning" that's actually "micro-teaching." Five-minute modules sound appealing but often sacrifice depth for brevity. There's a reason California requires a minimum of one hour: some things can't be meaningfully covered in the time it takes to microwave a burrito.

Celebrity-hosted training. I've seen vendors market their training based on the celebrity narrator. Your employees don't need a famous person telling them not to harass their coworkers. They need an expert who understands California law.

"Gamified" training that's actually just multiple choice with points. Adding a leaderboard to a quiz doesn't make it interactive. Building a response to a harassment disclosure word by word, or sorting workplace conduct into legal categories, or identifying red flags in a realistic Slack conversation: that's interactive.

One-size-fits-all national training. California's laws are different from federal law and different from other states. Your training needs to reflect that. Period.

The bottom line

The training you choose reflects what your company actually thinks about harassment prevention. If you pick the cheapest, fastest option that checks the box, your employees will notice, and they'll calibrate their expectations accordingly. If you pick training that's genuinely thoughtful, that presents real scenarios with real complexity, and that treats the learner like an adult, that sends a message too.
