California SB 1343 Requirements: The Complete 2026 Employer Guide

If you're reading this, you're probably an HR manager who just Googled "SB 1343 requirements" because someone on your leadership team asked whether the company is compliant, and you weren't 100% sure. I've been on both sides of that conversation. As a workplace investigator who's conducted over 500 investigations, I've seen what happens when companies get training right. I've also seen what happens when they don't. (Spoiler: I end up involved.)

Here's everything you need to know.

What SB 1343 Actually Requires

SB 1343 expanded California's existing harassment prevention training mandate (which previously only applied to supervisors at companies with 50+ employees) to cover virtually every employer in the state. If you have 5 or more employees, you're covered. That includes temps, part-timers, and independent contractors who count toward your headcount.

The basics: every non-supervisory employee needs at least one hour of training. Every supervisor needs at least two hours. New hires must be trained within six months. After that, it's every two years. (If you also have New York employees, heads up: New York requires annual training. Don't mix up your state deadlines.)

The Training Content Requirements Most Employers Miss

Here's where it gets more specific than most people realize. The California Code of Regulations, specifically 2 CCR § 11024, lays out exactly what your training must include. Not "should." Must.

The big ones that trip employers up:

The harassment definition under FEHA, not just Title VII. California uses a broader standard than federal law. Under FEHA, harassment can be "severe OR pervasive," not "severe AND pervasive" like the old federal standard. That one word changes the entire analysis. A single sufficiently severe incident can constitute harassment in California. Your training needs to explain this distinction explicitly.

Abusive conduct. AB 2053 added a requirement that training cover "abusive conduct," meaning workplace bullying that's malicious and unrelated to legitimate business interests. Here's the catch: abusive conduct isn't independently actionable under FEHA. You can't sue for bullying alone. But when bullying targets a protected category, it becomes harassment. Your training needs to explain that line clearly, not just check the box.

Bystander intervention. This one was added later and a lot of legacy training platforms still treat it as an afterthought. Your training must include practical strategies for bystander intervention, not just "speak up" but actual frameworks employees can use in the moment.

Remedies and complaint processes. Employees need to know about the CRD (formerly DFEH), the EEOC, their internal complaint process, and their right to file a complaint. Training that skips the agency contact information (800-884-1684 for the CRD) isn't compliant.

Supervisor-specific content. The two-hour supervisor track isn't just the one-hour training with an extra hour tacked on. Supervisors need specific training on their obligation to report, their personal liability under Gov. Code § 12940(j)(3), handling complaints, preventing retaliation, and the legal consequences of failing to act. If your training treats supervisors and non-supervisors the same, it's not compliant.

The Recordkeeping Requirement Nobody Talks About

SB 513 added recordkeeping requirements that took effect in 2020, and I'm consistently surprised by how many employers don't know about them. For every employee trained, you need to document: the employee's name, the training date, the training provider, the duration, core competencies covered, and any resulting certification.

These records must be maintained on the employee's personnel file. If the CRD comes knocking, or a plaintiff's attorney subpoenas your training records during litigation, "we did the training but didn't keep records" is not a defense. It's an admission.

What "Interactive" Actually Means

Here's one that catches a lot of employers using those cheap click-through video platforms. California requires that training be "interactive." Under the regulations, that means employees must have an opportunity to ask questions and receive answers. A passive video with a multiple-choice quiz at the end doesn't meet this standard. The regulations specifically contemplate scenarios, exercises, and real engagement, not a compliance video playing in a background tab while someone does their actual work.

If your employees are completing their "one hour" of training in 12 minutes, you have a problem.

What About the Harassment Standard? A Quick Primer.

Since your training needs to cover this accurately, here's what you need to know about how California evaluates harassment.

FEHA uses a "severe OR pervasive" standard, broader than the federal "severe AND pervasive" test. That "or" matters. California's Supreme Court addressed single incidents in Gov. Code § 12923(b): a single incident of harassing conduct is sufficient to create a triable issue regarding the existence of a hostile work environment. One incident can be enough.

Retaliation is evaluated under the "materiality" standard from Yanowitz v. L'Oreal: an employer action is retaliatory if it materially affects the terms, conditions, or privileges of employment. Your training needs to use California's standard specifically, not the federal standard or another state's. I've reviewed training content that got this wrong.

How to Choose a Training Provider

I'm biased (I built one) but here's what I'd tell any employer shopping for training, regardless of who they choose.

Ask to see their compliance mapping. If a vendor can't show you exactly which regulatory requirements their training satisfies, that's a red flag. There are 54 specific requirements across SB 1343, the implementing regulations, and SB 513. Your vendor should be able to map each one to a specific feature or content element in their training. If they say "we're compliant" but can't show you the mapping, keep shopping.

Check who created the content. There's a difference between training designed by employment attorneys who've actually investigated harassment cases and training designed by an instructional design team that Googled the statute. The scenarios should feel real, because they should be based on real workplace situations, not hypothetical textbook examples.

Test the interactivity. Go through the training yourself. If you can click through it in 15 minutes, your employees will too, and you're paying for a checkbox, not training. Look for decision scenarios where there's no obvious right answer, exercises that require genuine thought, and feedback that explains the legal nuance, not just "correct" and "incorrect."

Verify the recordkeeping. Your training platform should generate completion certificates, track time spent, export audit-ready records with all the SB 513 fields, and handle renewal reminders. If you're manually tracking who completed training in a spreadsheet, you're doing it wrong and you're going to miss someone.

A Note on Timing

If you're reading this and realizing you're behind, here's the practical reality: the CRD isn't going door-to-door checking training records. But plaintiff's attorneys absolutely are. In harassment cases, one of the first discovery requests is "produce all training records for the complainant and the accused." If those records show the accused supervisor never completed training, or completed a 12-minute click-through six months late, that becomes exhibit A in the case against your company.

Training is cheap. Litigation is not. The math isn't complicated.
